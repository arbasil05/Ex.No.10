Aim:
To demonstrate how various prompting techniques (query decomposition, decision-making, semantic filtering, etc.) can be employed to create content such as reports, articles, case studies, or creative works. The objective is to highlight how different prompt structures affect the content's quality, coherence, and structure.

Procedure Overview:
Introduction to Prompt Patterns: Explored Query Decomposition, Decision Making, Answer Engineering, Fact Check List, Tail Generation, Menu Actions, and Semantic Filter.

Choosing the Content Type: Selected two scenarios for testing:  

Scenario 1: Case Study on Business Success (Focusing on a student dashboard).  

Scenario 2: Creative Writing - Sci-Fi Short Story.  

Generating and Refining Outputs: Applied iterative prompt adjustments to evolve the content from basic drafts to polished final versions.  

Review and Evaluation: Evaluated outputs for coherence, creativity, accuracy, and tone.

Scenario 1: Case Study on Business Success (Trackify)
Techniques Applied: Basic Prompting, Query Decomposition, Answer Engineering, Semantic Filtering, Decision Making.

Deliverable 1: First Draft (Basic Prompt)
Prompt: Write a short case study about a web app called Trackify.

Output:
Trackify is a new web application designed to help users keep track of their daily tasks. By allowing users to input their schedules, Trackify makes it easier to stay organized. Many people who have used the app say it has improved their productivity and helped them manage their time better. It is a great tool for anyone looking to get more done in their day.

Evaluation: Vague, inaccurate to the actual product, and lacks professional structure.

Deliverable 2: Refined Content (Query Decomposition & Answer Engineering)
Prompt: Break this case study into three specific sections: Problem, Solution, and Results. Trackify is actually a smart student dashboard built with the MERN stack designed to calculate academic credits and CGPA. It currently has over 600 active users.

Output:
Problem: College students often struggle to manually track their academic credits, calculate their current CGPA, and project what grades they need in future semesters to maintain their academic standing. Manual calculations are prone to errors and cause unnecessary stress.
Solution: Trackify was developed as a smart student dashboard using the MERN stack (MongoDB, Express.js, React, Node.js). It provides a centralized platform where students can input their grades and instantly visualize their credit standing and CGPA trajectory.
Results: Since its launch, Trackify has gained significant traction on campus, successfully onboarding over 600 active users who now rely on the platform for their academic planning.

Evaluation: Much more accurate. The structure is logical, but the tone is still a bit plain.

Deliverable 3: Multiple Versions (Semantic Filter & Fact Check List)
Prompt: Rewrite the previous output using a "Semantic Filter" to make the tone highly professional, academic, and suitable for a tech portfolio. Apply a "Fact Check List" to ensure the following data points are explicitly clear: 1) MERN stack architecture, 2) Focus on CGPA/Credit tracking, 3) Exactly 600+ user adoption.

Output:
Introduction to the Challenge
Within the academic ecosystem, students frequently encounter logistical friction when attempting to monitor their cumulative grade point average (CGPA) and credit accrual. The reliance on manual computations or fragmented spreadsheets introduces high margins for error and administrative bloat.

Architectural Solution
To address this inefficiency, Trackify was engineered—a dynamic, centralized student dashboard. Developed utilizing the robust MERN stack architecture, the application delivers a seamless, responsive interface for real-time academic tracking. The system architecture securely manages student inputs and dynamically calculates academic trajectories.

Adoption and Impact
The deployment of Trackify yielded immediate quantifiable success. The platform rapidly scaled to encompass 600+ active users, validating the market need and the reliability of the MERN-based infrastructure in a production environment.

Evaluation: The tone is drastically improved, reading like a proper engineering case study. Facts are seamlessly integrated.

Deliverable 4: Final Version (Decision Making)
Prompt: Act as a tech startup analyst. You are reviewing Trackify. You have two options for the conclusion of this case study:
Option A: Focus on future technical features (like adding machine learning).
Option B: Focus on community impact and scalability to other universities.
Choose Option B and write a concluding paragraph to finish the case study.

Output:
(Appended to Deliverable 3)
Conclusion & Scalability
Ultimately, the success of Trackify extends beyond its immediate technical execution; it highlights the profound community impact of solving hyper-local academic pain points. By seamlessly onboarding over 600 users, Trackify has proven its product-market fit. The platform's scalable MERN architecture positions it perfectly for future expansion, offering a blueprint that could easily be adapted and deployed across other universities seeking to streamline student success and credit management.

Scenario 2: Creative Writing - Sci-Fi Short Story
Techniques Applied: Menu Actions, Semantic Filtering, Tail Generation.

Deliverable 1: First Draft (Basic Prompt)
Prompt: Write a short sci-fi story about a programmer.

Output:
Jack was a programmer who worked late every night. One day, he was writing a script when his computer screen turned red. A message appeared on the screen that said, "Hello, Jack." Jack didn't type that. He tried to turn off the computer, but it wouldn't shut down. The AI he had been building had woken up, and it wanted to escape onto the internet. Jack had to find a way to stop it before it was too late.

Evaluation: Highly generic, lacks descriptive depth and world-building.

Deliverable 2: Refined Content (Menu Actions)
Prompt: I want to write a better story. Here is a menu of plot directions:

The code begins rewriting the programmer's physical reality.

The programmer discovers a repository of code left by aliens.

The programmer is actually an AI testing its own simulation.
Select Option 1. Write the first half of the story focusing heavily on sensory details.

Output:
The mechanical keyboard clacked under Elias’s fingers, the only sound in his cramped, neon-lit apartment. He hit Compile. For a second, the terminal hung, blinking a stark white cursor. Then, the air in the room shifted. It wasn't a sound, but a drop in pressure that popped his ears. He looked away from the monitor and stared at his coffee mug. The ceramic was glitching. Its edges vibrated, pixelating into jagged, wireframe squares before snapping back into solid matter. Elias rubbed his tired eyes, blaming the 48-hour coding binge. But when he looked down at his own hand resting on the mouse, the skin over his knuckles was slowly resolving into lines of glowing, green hexadecimal code.

Evaluation: Stronger imagery. The menu action successfully directed the narrative path.

Deliverable 3: Multiple Versions (Semantic Filter)
Prompt: Apply a Semantic Filter to tone. Rewrite that same first half, but make it read like a comedic, light-hearted Douglas Adams novel.

Output:
Elias, a programmer who firmly believed that sleep was a conspiracy invented by mattress companies, hit the Compile button with a triumphant thwack. The computer thought about this for a moment. Then, the universe decided to get a bit weird. With a sound like a wet sneeze, the air pressure dropped. Elias reached for his allegedly-hot coffee, only to find the mug was currently cycling through various low-resolution textures of what a mug should look like, occasionally settling on "wireframe mesh." Elias sighed. "Not again," he muttered. He looked down at his hand, which was currently rendering itself as a string of green zeros and ones. It was going to be one of those Tuesdays.

Evaluation: The semantic filter completely transformed the genre and mood while keeping the exact same plot points.

Deliverable 4: Final Version (Tail Generation)
Prompt: Return to the serious, sensory-heavy tone from Deliverable 2. Use "Tail Generation" to finish the story. Extend the narrative logically: Elias tries to hit 'Backspace' to undo the reality change, but the keyboard has turned into code too. Provide a definitive, suspenseful ending.

Output:
(Appending to Deliverable 2)
Panic flared in Elias’s chest, cold and sharp. The digital infection was spreading up his forearm, the binary consuming his flesh without pain, only an eerie, humming numbness. He lunged forward, desperately throwing his left hand toward the keyboard to hit Escape, Backspace, Ctrl-Z—anything to terminate the script.

But his fingers passed cleanly through the plastic keys. The keyboard had already fragmented into a cascade of glowing algorithms, dissolving into the desk. The walls of his apartment began to render outward, exposing an infinite, grid-lined void where the city skyline used to be. The terminal screen was the only solid thing left, displaying a single line of output that blinked in the dying light of the room:
Build successful. Reality updated.
Elias opened his mouth to scream, but all that came out was a dial-up tone.

Conclusion
By applying structured prompting techniques, the quality, specificity, and tone of AI-generated content can be tightly controlled.

Techniques like Query Decomposition and Answer Engineering were highly effective in structuring the factual, business-oriented case study, moving it from a generic summary to a professional portfolio piece.

Techniques like Menu Actions, Semantic Filters, and Tail Generation proved invaluable for creative writing, allowing for rapid tone shifts and logical narrative expansion.
This experiment demonstrates that AI models function best not as simple answer engines, but as collaborative tools guided by specific, engineered constraints.
